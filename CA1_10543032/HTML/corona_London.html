The first COVID-19 case in London, England, was confirmed on 12 February 2020 in a woman who had recently arrived from China, where the 2019\xe2\x80\x9320 coronavirus pandemic began. \n

As of 10 April 2020, London is one of the worst affected regions of England, with 16,011 cases,[1] and 2,369 deaths.[2]\n

History[edit]

\n

The first confirmed case of COVID-19 in London was detected on 12 February 2020, in a woman who had arrived from China with the virus a few days earlier. She was the ninth case in the UK.[3]\n

By 17 March, there had been almost 500 confirmed cases in London and 23 deaths, and Mayor of London Sadiq Khan announced the London Underground would begin running a reduced service due to the virus.[4] A day earlier UK Prime Minister Boris Johnson had stated that London\'s outbreak was a few weeks ahead of the rest of the UK.[5] The city had seen the most cases of any UK region, with numbers rising much faster than elsewhere in the UK.[6]\n

On 18 March, Southwark and Westminster were the worst affected boroughs.[7] By 10 April, the five worst affected boroughs were Brent (912 cases), Croydon (853), Barnet (837), Southwark (826) and Lambeth (794).[1]\n

\n

Response by sector[edit]

\n

NHS[edit]

\n
NHS Nightingale Hospital London
\n

With a high number of cases, older populations and lower number of pre-Covid-19 critical care beds, district general hospitals, located around the centre of London were found in an analysis by Edge Health, to have experienced significant demand.[8] On the evening of 19 March, Northwick Park Hospital in Harrow said it had no critical care capacity left and was contacting neighbouring hospitals about transferring patients who need critical care to other sites.[9] On 4 April, Watford General Hospital also declared a \'critical incident\'.[10]\n

Meanwhile, on 24 March, health secretary Matt Hancock said that a temporary new hospital, the NHS Nightingale Hospital, was to be opened in the ExCeL Centre in east London the following week. This critical care hospital would provide up to 4,000 extra critical care beds for London.[11] The London hospital opened on 3 April,[12] and received its first patients on 7 April.[13]\n

\n

Public transport[edit]

\n
Stay at home and social distancing notices London Underground March 2020
\n

The Waterloo & City line, several tube stations and the Night Tube were suspended on 19 March,[14] with the Mayor of London and Transport for London (TfL) urging people to only use public transport if absolutely essential so that it can be used by critical workers.[15] From 20 March, 40 tube stations were closed.[16]\n

On 20 March, Southeastern became the first train operating company to announce a reduced timetable, which would come into use from 23 March.[17] From 30 March, the Gatwick Express service was suspended.[18]\n

The London Underground brought in new measures on 25 March to combat the spread of the virus, by slowing the flow of passengers onto platforms. Measures included the imposition of queuing at ticket gates and turning off some escalators.[19]\n

On 25 March, London City Airport announced it would temporarily close due to the coronavirus outbreak.[20] Heathrow Airport closed one runway from 6 April, while Gatwick Airport closed one of its two terminals, and said its runway would open for scheduled flights only between 2pm and 10pm.[21]\n

\n

Education[edit]

\n

By 17 March most universities in London had suspended face-to-face learning or were planning to do so by 23 March, many moving classes online.[22] It was announced on 18 March that schools in London would close by 20 March, as in the rest of the UK.[23]\n
